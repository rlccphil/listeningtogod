Theme 1

Listening to God

Spiritual leadership is not primarily leading for God, but leading with God. And leading with God requires the discipline of listening.

"Even Jesus did not minister from constant activity,
but from continual communion." — A Central Truth
📖

Moses

Before confronting Pharaoh, Moses listened. The burning bush was not a disruption — it was an invitation to hear the voice of God before acting in God's name.

👂

Samuel

"Speak, Lord, for your servant is listening." (1 Samuel 3:10) Samuel's ministry was shaped not by what he wanted to do for God, but by what he heard from God.

🕊️

Jesus

Jesus repeatedly withdrew to pray and listen to the Father. Even the Son of God — fully present in every teaching and miracle — rooted his ministry in communion, not merely activity.

When a leader stops listening to God, something subtle but serious happens: ministry becomes mechanical. Without an attentive ear:

  • 🔹 We become reactive rather than responsive
  • 🔹 We lead from anxiety rather than trust
  • 🔹 We rely only on technique, not discernment
  • 🔹 Ministry becomes obligation, not communion
"A leader who stops listening to God eventually begins listening only to pressure, expectations, and fear."

True spiritual leaders are distinguished not by how much they accomplish, but by the quality of their inner life. Those who listen deeply are:

  • ✦ Less driven by ego and the need to impress
  • ✦ Less enslaved by approval and performance
  • ✦ More surrendered to God's leading, even in uncertainty
"Why, my soul, are you downcast? Why so disturbed within me? Put your hope in God, for I will yet praise him, my Savior and my God." Psalm 42:5

The psalmist is emotionally honest before God. This is where discernment begins — not in certainty, but in honesty.

Contemplative Pause

  • What would change in your leadership if listening came first?
  • Where have you been leading from anxiety rather than communion?

Theme 2

Spiritual Leadership

What makes a shepherd a spiritual shepherd? Not primarily skill, strategy, or even zeal — but attentiveness to the voice of the One who calls.

"Discernment begins with honesty. The psalmist does not pretend. He brings his downcast soul, his disturbance, his questions — and lays them before God."

The Difference That Changes Everything

Many pastors speak about God with great eloquence. Far fewer have learned to walk with God in the dailiness of ministry. The difference is not in knowledge — it is in attentiveness.

Spiritual leadership flows from a listening posture. It is leadership that:

  • 🌿 Waits before it acts
  • 🌿 Notices before it speaks
  • 🌿 Discerns before it decides
  • 🌿 Prays before it plans
"I am the good shepherd; I know my sheep and my sheep know me — just as the Father knows me and I know the Father — and I lay down my life for the sheep." John 10:14–15
"The good shepherd's knowledge of the sheep flows from the shepherd's knowledge of the Father. To shepherd well, one must first listen well."

Holy Noticing

Spiritual leadership requires developing the capacity to notice — not just with the mind but with the whole self. Three questions can orient a leader throughout the day:

What is happening inside me right now?

What might God be saying through this?

What draws me toward love, faith, and hope?

Theme 3

Obstacles to Listening

Many leaders genuinely want to hear God — but subtle forces work against a listening posture. Naming them honestly is itself an act of spiritual courage.

The obstacle is not primarily external — it is the internal noise that accumulates from constant information, hurry, and the addiction to productivity.

"God's first language is silence." — Thomas Keating

We have become fluent in a second language — noise, speed, output — and have grown awkward in the mother tongue of God.

Many leaders — especially those trained in ministry — have learned to suppress what they feel. Grief, anger, fear, and disappointment are pushed down in order to continue functioning.

But ignored emotions do not disappear. They continue to shape how we lead, react, make decisions, and relate to those in our care.

Psalm 42 offers a different way: the psalmist does not silence his emotions before God — he brings them, names them, and holds them in the presence of God. This is the beginning of listening.

"Why, my soul, are you downcast? Why so disturbed within me?" Psalm 42:5a

Sometimes we do not listen because, somewhere beneath the surface, we have already decided. We want confirmation, not revelation. We seek permission, not direction.

Listening to God requires something threatening to leadership identity: relinquishing control.

"Listening requires relinquishing control. The one who must always know what God is saying has likely stopped truly listening."

Some leaders fear silence because productivity validates their identity. To be still is to be unproductive. To be unproductive is to be unworthy.

But God's love is not earned through ministry performance. Silence does not expose emptiness — it often reveals the fullness of a Presence we have been too busy to notice.

The invitation of this retreat is to return to belovedness — to remember that you are a beloved child before you are a capable leader.

Ignatian Spirituality

Consolation & Desolation

Ignatius of Loyola — not as a theological system, but as a gift to the soul — gave us language for what the heart already knows: that God is active in our inner movements.

What Ignatius Observed

After his wounding at Pamplona, Ignatius had time to reflect. He noticed something: certain thoughts and imaginings left him restless and dry. Others left him with a deep, quiet peace.

From this, he developed a vocabulary for the spiritual life — one that helps us notice not just what we think, but how the Spirit moves within us.

✦ Consolation

Where recently have you sensed…

  • Life and energy
  • Deep peace
  • Joy or gratitude
  • Deepened faith
  • Draw toward God
  • Clarity and hope

🌑 Desolation

Where recently have you sensed…

  • Heaviness or dryness
  • Confusion or fog
  • Discouragement
  • Spiritual emptiness
  • Pull away from God
  • Restlessness or fear

Ignatius was not teaching mysticism — he was teaching attentiveness. Both consolation and desolation are invitations: one to receive gratefully, the other to notice and bring honestly to God.

Important Caution

Consolation is not the same as comfort, and desolation is not the same as sin. A leader in desolation is not abandoned. God is often most present in what feels most dry.

"In times of desolation, one should never make a change, but rather hold firm." — Ignatius of Loyola

Theme 4

Discernment Practices

Discernment is not a technique — it is a disposition. It is the cultivated art of recognizing God's movements in ordinary life, in Scripture, in people, in silence.

"Discernment is learning to pay attention to God's activity in ordinary life."
👁️

Finding God in All Things

Ignatius believed God is not confined to church services, sermons, or spiritual disciplines. He is active in conversations, interruptions, suffering, desires, relationships, and ministry tensions. The question is not "Where is God?" but "Will I notice?"

🌱

Noticing Inner Movements

Before acting on a decision or impulse, pause: Does this movement lead toward love, hope, and trust? Or toward anxiety, self-protection, and control? The direction of the movement matters.

🤫

Silence Before Decisions

Many decisions that seemed urgent could wait. Discerning leaders develop the practice of sitting quietly before God before responding — even for five minutes — to allow clarity to emerge.

📖

Lectio Divina

Sacred reading — not studying Scripture for information, but receiving it as a word addressed to you, right now. Read a passage slowly three times: What word or phrase catches your attention? What does God seem to be saying to you through it?

✍️

Reflective Journaling

Journaling for discernment is different from journaling as a diary. It involves asking: "What do I notice inside me? What might God be doing here?" Over time, patterns emerge that reveal how God speaks and moves in your particular soul.

Discernment Exercise — "What might God be revealing?"

Look at your consolation and desolation reflections from the previous section. With those in view, consider this question:

Ignatian Practice

The Daily Examen

The Examen is not merely reviewing your day. It is learning to notice God's presence in your day — the invitations you may have missed, the graces you may have overlooked.

What the Examen Is Not

The Examen is not a performance review. It is not a guilt exercise. It does not ask, "How well did I do?" It asks, "Where was God, and did I notice?" This shifts the posture from self-evaluation to attentive receptivity.

Interactive Examen — Five Movements
Movement 1 of 5

Gratitude

Begin not with your failures or questions, but with thanks. Rest for a moment in the simple awareness that God has been present in this day.

"Give thanks to the Lord, for He is good; His love endures forever."Psalm 107:1
0 words
Movement 2 of 5

Review the Day

Walk slowly through your day — not to judge it, but to see it with fresh eyes. Where were the moments of grace? Where was energy given or drained?

0 words
Movement 3 of 5

Notice Emotions

What emotions were most present today? God often speaks through what we feel, not just what we think. Name them without judgment.

Emotions are not distractions from the spiritual life — they are often its very language.

0 words
Movement 4 of 5

Discern God's Invitations

As you look at this day, where do you sense God has been present? What might He have been inviting you toward? What did you perhaps resist or overlook?

Movement 5 of 5

Respond in Prayer

Close the Examen not with more reflection, but with prayer. Speak to God freely. Offer what you've noticed — your gratitude, your failures, your questions, your longings.
